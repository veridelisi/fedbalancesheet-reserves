Companion Guide · Policy Option 1

Recognize Discount Window Capacity in the Liquidity Coverage Ratio

A plain-language breakdown of the proposal, how it works, and the math behind estimated balance sheet reduction of $50–$450 billion.

The Proposal

What Is Being Proposed?

Currently, when banks calculate their Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR), they must show they hold enough high-quality, immediately available assets (called HQLA) to cover a full 30 days of cash outflows under a stress scenario. The discount window — the Federal Reserve's emergency lending facility — is completely excluded from this calculation.

Policy Option 1 proposes a straightforward change: allow banks to count pre-positioned discount window borrowing capacity toward their LCR numerator, subject to a cap (proposed at 20% of total HQLA). "Pre-positioned" means collateral already pledged to the Fed before a crisis occurs — the bank has done the paperwork in advance, so borrowing would be fast and reliable if needed.

The core idea in one sentence: If a bank has already pledged loan collateral at the discount window and can demonstrably borrow from the Fed in a crisis, regulators should count that borrowing capacity as a form of liquidity — not require the bank to hold additional cash or Treasuries on top of it.

What is the LCR?

A post-2008 rule requiring banks (over $100B in assets) to hold enough liquid assets to survive 30 days of stress. The ratio must stay at or above 100% (85%–70% for smaller banks).

What is the Discount Window?

The Fed's lending facility for banks facing short-term liquidity shortfalls. Banks borrow against collateral (often loans). It has a stigma problem — banks avoid it for fear of appearing weak.

What is HQLA?

High-Quality Liquid Assets — the numerator of the LCR. Mainly reserves, Treasury securities, and Agency MBS. Currently the discount window does not count toward HQLA.

The Problem

Why Does This Matter?

Banks subject to the LCR collectively held $4.1 trillion in HQLA as of 2025. A large portion of that is parked as reserves at the Federal Reserve — not circulating in the broader economy. This is not accidental; it is a direct consequence of the LCR requiring pre-funded liquidity buffers.

The discount window is theoretically designed to solve exactly the problem that HQLA buffers solve: giving banks access to cash in a crisis. But banks cannot use discount window access to satisfy their LCR requirement. The result is a redundant liquidity layer — banks hold both a regulatory buffer and maintain access to the Fed's lending window, when either alone might suffice.

Current System
Banks must hold $4.1T in HQLA (cash, Treasuries, reserves) entirely on their balance sheet. Discount window capacity — even if pre-positioned — counts for nothing in the LCR calculation. Banks hold both large HQLA buffers and pre-pledged collateral at the discount window.
Proposed System
Banks with pre-pledged collateral at the discount window can count up to 20% of their HQLA requirement as satisfied by that borrowing capacity. This reduces the amount of actual reserves/Treasuries they must hold on the balance sheet, freeing up reserves to drain from the system.

The stigma problem: Banks also avoid using the discount window even in genuine need, because usage signals distress. This proposal nudges banks toward regular pre-positioning, which could normalize the facility and reduce stigma over time — a secondary benefit beyond balance sheet reduction.

Mechanism

How Does It Work Step by Step?

1

Bank pre-pledges loan collateral at the Fed

A bank goes through the administrative process of pledging its loan portfolio (e.g., mortgages, business loans) as collateral at its regional Federal Reserve Bank. After haircuts for credit risk, this creates a determined borrowing capacity — e.g., a bank might have $100B in collateral that translates to $70B in available discount window borrowing.

2

Regulators recognize this capacity in the LCR numerator

Under the new rule, that $70B in pre-positioned capacity could count toward HQLA — up to a cap of 20% of the bank's total HQLA pool. If the bank's total HQLA is $350B, the cap is $70B, meaning the full capacity could be applied.

3

Bank reduces its on-balance-sheet reserves

Because the bank's LCR now shows sufficient liquid coverage (partly through discount window capacity), it no longer needs to hold as many reserves or Treasury securities. It can allow reserves to drain — either by not replacing maturing assets or by deploying funds into loans.

4

Reserves decline system-wide → Fed's balance sheet shrinks

As banks across the system reduce reserve holdings, aggregate reserves fall. This is a reduction in the Fed's liabilities. Since assets and liabilities must balance, the Fed can also let its security holdings decline — meaning the balance sheet itself shrinks.

Simplified flow: how the change moves through the system
Banks pre-pledge loan collateral at Discount Window
→
Discount window capacity recognized in LCR numerator (up to 20% cap)
→
Banks need less on-balance-sheet HQLA (reserves/Treasuries)
→
System-wide reserves fall → Fed balance sheet shrinks
Mathematical Explanation

The Math Behind the Estimate

The paper walks through a four-step calculation to arrive at the $50B–$450B range. Here is each step made explicit.

Step 1 — The LCR Formula

Current LCR requirement (unchanged)
HQLA (reserves + Treasuries + Agency MBS) Net Cash Outflows over 30-day stress period ≥ 100%
HQLA = High-Quality Liquid Assets held on balance sheet today
NCO = Net Cash Outflows; computed with fixed regulatory run-off rates

Step 2 — Maximum Theoretical Relief

LCR-subject banks collectively hold $4.1 trillion in HQLA (2025 data). The proposed cap allows discount window capacity to substitute for up to 20% of that total:

Maximum regulatory relief (if cap fully utilized)
$4,100B × 20% = $820 billion
This is the gross upper bound on HQLA that could theoretically be replaced by discount window capacity.

Note: The paper confirms that current pre-pledged collateral (after haircuts) at the discount window already exceeds $1.6 trillion — more than double the $820B cap. So no new collateral pledging is required; this is purely a regulatory accounting change for capacity already in place.

Step 3 — The Reserve Share Adjustment

Not all HQLA is reserves. Banks spread HQLA across reserves (~one-third), Treasuries, and Agency MBS. Reducing the LCR requirement does not force banks to reduce reserves specifically — they can reduce any form of HQLA. The paper centers its estimate on one-third of the maximum relief accruing as reserve reduction:

Reserve-specific reduction (midpoint estimate)
$820B × ~⅓ ≈ $273 billion
Rounded to $250B as the midpoint estimate given implementation uncertainty.

Step 4 — Uncertainty Range and Final Estimate

The paper applies substantial uncertainty bounds around the midpoint to account for: (a) only partial take-up of the 20% cap by banks; (b) other regulations (e.g., the NSFR) potentially becoming binding; (c) banks choosing to reduce Treasuries rather than reserves. The final range is:

Minimum
$50B
Midpoint
$250B
Maximum
$450B
Range reflects a uniform probability distribution over possible outcomes. Aggregate totals use Monte Carlo simulation across all policy options (1,000,000 draws).

Supporting Evidence from Academic Studies

Nicolae (2020)

Estimates the LCR's introduction shifted U.S. reserve demand outward by roughly 1.5% of GDP — equivalent to ~$500B today — consistent with the paper's upper estimate.

Kedan & Ventula Vidal (2021)

Found the LCR increased Eurozone reserve demand by €200B (~10% of ECB's balance sheet at the time). Scaling that 10% to today's Fed balance sheet implies ~$600B — again close to the upper bound.

Key Caveats

What Could Limit the Effect?

NSFR Interaction

If banks shift from holding reserves to pledging loans at the discount window, the Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR) denominator rises — loans require more stable funding than reserves. NSFR could become the new binding constraint, eating into potential gains unless it is also recalibrated.

Intraday Needs

Banks must still hold reserves to process payments throughout the day (intraday clearing needs). Even if the LCR is satisfied with discount window capacity, a floor of reserve demand remains for operational reasons — limiting how far reserves can actually fall.

Partial Take-Up

Banks may not fully utilize the 20% cap — some may prefer the simplicity and certainty of holding reserves over the operational complexity of maintaining pre-pledged collateral relationships with the Fed.

Stigma Reduction Required

The full benefit only materializes if banks genuinely believe they can and will use the discount window in a stress event without market consequences. Persistent stigma around the facility could undermine banks' willingness to reduce their reserve buffers even after the regulatory change.

Summary

Bottom Line

Policy Option 1 is a regulatory accounting reform, not a change to how the discount window itself operates. It recognizes a logical inconsistency in the current LCR framework: banks are simultaneously required to hold large liquid buffers and maintain access to the Fed's lender-of-last-resort facility — when the design intention was always that the two could substitute for each other in a crisis.

By allowing pre-positioned discount window capacity to count in the LCR (up to 20% of HQLA), banks gain relief to reduce on-balance-sheet reserves. System-wide, the paper estimates this could reduce reserve demand by $50B to $450B, making it one of the larger individual contributors in the 15-option policy catalog.

This is option 1 of 15. The paper's full Monte Carlo simulation estimates that all options combined could shrink the Fed's balance sheet by $1.2 to $2.1 trillion. Policy Option 1 alone accounts for roughly 15–20% of that potential at the midpoint, making it the single largest regulatory lever identified.
